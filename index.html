    Problem #2: "This Google search is giving me too many results."
    Solution: Advanced search can narrow it down in useful ways.

    Some of yall probably know about advanced search:
    https://www.google.com/advanced_search
    You can narrow searches by when the site was last updated, by phrases you don't want, etc.
    You can also do it from the search bar, which is nice because you can ask for any filetype, not just the ones listed on the advanced search page:

    "tom wolfe" -book
    = search for the exact phrase "tom wolfe," but leave out any results that contain the word "book"

    georgia history site:edu
    = search for the words "georgia" and "history", but only in websites that end in ".edu"

    landfills site:georgia.gov filetype:xls
    = search for the word "landfills" in all websites that end in georiga.gov, but only show me Excel spreadsheets (.xls) TEST3 (Sometimes the foot of the page will ask if you want to show omitted results. Yes. Show those.)


    Problem #3: I've got a data table, but I'm counting up things by hand. How can I summarize data in Excel?
    Solution: Folks, the Excel Pivot Table.
    **to insert here: a youtube screen video of me doing this
    Let's start with lotto.xls, a list of where lottery winners live, how much they won and when they won it.

    1. Open the file in Excel and find "Pivot Table." In most versions, it's under Data -> Pivot Table




    2. You've clicked "OK" and you are taken to a new page with a black box at the side.
    In that black box, drag "city" down to "row labels"
    Go back up to the top and grab "city" again and drag it down to "values"
    Voila, a count of how many winners come from each city



    3. How about the sum collected by folk in each city?
    In that black box, in the bottom right where it says "count of city," grab that and pull it out of the black box to make it disappear
    Then at the top, grab "amount_won" and pull it down to "Values"



    4. For extra fun, drag "year_won" down to "Row Labels" too




    Problem #4: "This document is a stupid .pdf. It contains tables I want to open in Excel."
    Solution: Cometdocs.
    PDFs are kind of like a picture of words and tables. Sometimes you can search the text in a .pdf. But sometimes you can't. And you certainly can't open a .pdf with Excel.
    So there are a couple of services out there that unlock the text and tables in .pdfs.
    It's prone to error because a computer is making its best guess at what it's seeing.
    IMHO, Cometdocs gives you a really clean conversion at a pretty good price.
    ???*** TBC, still free subscription with IRE membership???***
    You can convert a few docs a month with a free subscription
    With a paid subscription, you can do more conversions and "OCR" the text.
    (OCR: Verb. To turn pictures of words into actual searchable text.)

    Problem #6: "This file is too large to send/recieve via email."
    Solution: Sign up for Dropbox.
    This is an app that stores really big files for you. If somebody's email is rejecting a file from you, you can upload it to Dropbox and just send them a link to it.

    If they are trying to send you a doc that your email is rejecting, you can send them a link to your Dropbox, and they can send it there without having a Dropbox account.
